Space-based solar power (SBSP or SSP) refers to collecting solar energy in outer space via solar power satellites (SPS) and transmitting it to Earth. The concept offers higher energy collection due to lack of atmospheric interference, potential for continuous power generation, and better solar orientation.

## Key Technical Constraints

### Thermal Management
Waste heat disposal becomes intractable when spacecraft absorb maximum solar radiation; thermal control systems may interfere with panels or transmitters. (Space environment thermal management is a shared constraint with space data centers.)

### Launch Costs
The massive expense of satellite space placement is the primary challenge. Assuming 1 kg/kW solar panel mass, a 4 GW power station weighs ~4,000 metric tons—all requiring Earth launch.

Cost table (4 GW station, 1 kg/kW panels):
- $2,000/kg (Falcon Heavy): $8 billion
- $10,000/kg (Ariane V): $40 billion
Target: $100-200/kg to GEO for economic viability.

### Radiator Area
PV panels suffer ~8× greater degradation in space than on Earth. Thermal radiators required for waste heat rejection.

## Organizations and Projects

- **Caltech MAPLE (2023)**: Successfully demonstrated wireless power beaming to Earth (3.2W EIRP)
- **JAXA (2015)**: Wirelessly beamed 1.8 kW over 50 meters
- **Aetherflux**: Venture-funded ($50M), LEO laser constellation; pivoted to space-based data centers (December 2025)
- **China CAST**: Plans 200-tonne megawatt-grade station by 2035
- **UK Space Energy Initiative**: First launch mid-2040s
- **European Space Agency SOLARIS**: Proposed Solar Power Satellites from 2030
- **US Naval Research Laboratory**: First satellite solar power generation test (May 2020)

## Orbital Options

- **GEO (geostationary)**: Continuous power, but 1 km transmitting antenna → 10 km receiving area at Earth
- **LEO**: Lower launch energy, but requires constellations for continuous coverage; Kessler syndrome risk
- **MEO**: Proposed for in-space utilities

## Relevance to Space Data Centers

SBSP and space data centers share fundamental engineering constraints: radiator area limits, launch mass budgets, orbital mechanics, radiation hardening, and autonomous maintenance challenges. Aetherflux's December 2025 pivot to space data centers establishes a direct organizational link.
