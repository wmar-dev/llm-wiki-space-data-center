# Kessler syndrome

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kessler_syndrome
Fetched: 2026-06-07

The Kessler syndrome (also known as collisional cascading) is a scenario proposed by NASA scientists Donald J. Kessler and Burton G. Cour-Palais in 1978. It describes a situation where the density of objects in low Earth orbit (LEO) becomes so high that collisions cascade, exponentially increasing space debris. 

Key data points:
- In 2009, Kessler wrote that the debris environment had already become unstable
- The problem has three regimes: low density (decay > production), critical density (collisions add debris), high density (production > decay, cascading chain reaction)
- As of 2025, ~11,800 satellites in orbit, most (7,135) Starlink
- As of 2021, 600,000 pieces 1-10 cm, 23,000 larger
- In 2023, Bongers & Torres modeled a threshold of ~72,000 satellites to prevent Kessler syndrome (first approximation, no LEO/GEO distinction)
- Two LEO bands (900-1000 km and 1500 km) already past critical density per US National Academy of Sciences
- Kessler's 1991 paper: "Collisional cascading: The limits of population growth in low Earth orbit"
- Mitigation: graveyard orbits, laser broom, ClearSpace active debris removal, passivation of rocket stages
- 2024 Long March 6A breakup created 700+ fragments
- 2021 Russian ASAT (Kosmos 1408) created 1500 tracked fragments
- Solar storm analysis (2025): large solar storm could disable avoidance maneuvers for ~3 days, potentially triggering Kessler syndrome
- Solution to Fermi paradox: some astronomers suggest Kessler syndrome may trap spacefaring civilizations
