# An Orbital House of Cards: Frequent Megaconstellation Close Conjunctions

**Authors**: Sarah Thiele, Skye R. Heiland, Aaron C. Boley, Samantha M. Lawler
**arXiv**: 2512.09643
**Submitted**: December 10, 2025; revised January 8, 2026
**Category**: Earth and Planetary Astrophysics

## Abstract

The number of objects in orbit is rapidly increasing, primarily driven by the launch
of megaconstellations. Associated challenges include debris accumulation, collision
hazards, and atmospheric pollution.

## Key Innovation: CRASH Clock Metric

The authors introduce the CRASH Clock — a metric estimating the timeframe within which
a catastrophic collision could occur in the absence of active satellite maneuvering or
full situational awareness.

## Critical Quantitative Findings

- **Current CRASH Clock (2025)**: 5.5 days
- **CRASH Clock in 2018**: 164 days
- **Compression**: ~30× reduction in safety window over ~7 years

The dramatic decrease illustrates heightened collision vulnerability in the contemporary
orbital environment. The authors stress the urgency of developing improved methodologies
for assessing orbital stress, particularly regarding the cascading risks posed by
megaconstellation expansion and potential disruptive events like solar storms.

## Implications

Loss of maneuvering capability (e.g., from a solar storm or software failure) could
trigger a catastrophic collision within approximately 5.5 days under 2025 conditions.
In 2018, the same failure would have given 164 days before expected catastrophe.
