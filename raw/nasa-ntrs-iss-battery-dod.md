# International Space Station Lithium-Ion Battery

**Authors**: Penni J. Dalton (NASA Glenn Research Center), Sonia Balcer (Aerojet Rocketdyne)
**Source**: NASA NTRS 20160012048
**Published**: April 18, 2016
**URL**: https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20160012048

## Abstract

Describes the ISS Electric Power System's transition from aging Nickel-Hydrogen (Ni-H2) batteries to newer Lithium-Ion technology, including system architecture and development progress.

## Key Claims

- The Ni-H2 batteries are designed to operate at a **35% depth of discharge (DOD) maximum** during normal operation in Low Earth Orbit
- 48 total Ni-H2 Orbital Replacement Units in service; oldest units cycling since September 2006, approaching end-of-life
- Li-ion development initiative began in 2010 with concurrent cell life testing
- Batteries charge during sunlit orbital periods and discharge during eclipse phases — the fundamental LEO power cycle

## Significance

The 35% DoD constraint illustrates the severe cycling constraints on orbital battery systems. Operating at deeper discharge extends each cycle's energy but dramatically shortens battery life. The transition to Li-ion reflects the need for higher cycle life at acceptable DoD.
