## Summary

The Kessler syndrome describes a cascading collision scenario in low Earth orbit where debris density triggers a chain reaction, exponentially increasing the amount of space debris.

## Key Claims

- Kessler syndrome has three regimes: stable (decay > production), critical (collisions add debris), and cascading (production > decay). — *(other)*
- In 2009, Kessler stated the debris environment had already become unstable. — *(other)*
- Two LEO bands (900–1,000 km and 1,500 km) are already past critical density per the US National Academy of Sciences. — *(other)*
- Bongers & Torres (2023) estimated an aggregate threshold of ~72,000 satellites to prevent Kessler syndrome (first approximation, no LEO/GEO distinction). — *(other)*
- ~11,800 satellites in orbit as of 2025, most (7,135) Starlink. — *(other)*
- 600,000 pieces of debris 1–10 cm, 23,000 larger than 10 cm. — *(other)*
- One satellite is destroyed by collision per year on average. — *(other)*
- 2021 Russian ASAT (Kosmos 1408): 1,500 tracked fragments, hundreds of thousands untracked. — *(other)*
- 2024 Long March 6A breakup: 700+ fragments. — *(other)*
- 2025 solar storm analysis: large solar storm could neutralize avoidance maneuvers for ~3 days, potentially triggering Kessler syndrome. — *(other)*
- Proposed solutions: graveyard orbits, laser broom, active debris removal (ClearSpace), passivation. — *(other)*

## Domain Relevance

This article establishes the fundamental physical limit on orbital object density. It is directly relevant to space data center analysis: any large constellation of orbital data centers would increase debris density and risk of Kessler syndrome.
