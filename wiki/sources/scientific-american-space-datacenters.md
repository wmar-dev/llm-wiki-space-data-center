# Data Centers in Space

**Source**: Scientific American
**Source type**: news_article
**Origin URL**: https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/data-centers-in-space/

## Summary

Broad industry survey covering the main orbital data center projects, their economic requirements, environmental claims, and counterclaims. Introduces the Saarland University environmental study as a major counterpoint to carbon efficiency claims.

## Key Claims

**Companies and projects**:
- **Google Suncatcher**: solar-powered satellite constellation with AI chips; demo mission 2027; requires <$200/kg by 2035 for viability
- **Starcloud**: 60 kg satellite with NVIDIA H100 GPU; projects 5 GW orbital data center by 2035; claims 10× lower carbon emissions vs. gas-powered terrestrial facilities
- **China Xingshidai**: operating space data center constellation
- **EU ASCEND project**: studying the concept

**Technical requirements**:
- Launch costs must fall below $200/kg by 2035
- Onboard chips require replacement every **5–6 years** due to radiation and degradation
- Sun-synchronous orbit enables near-constant solar exposure

**Environmental counter-claim** (Saarland University, "Dirty Bits in Low-Earth Orbit"):
- Orbital data centers could generate **an order of magnitude greater emissions** than terrestrial facilities powered by the grid — from rocket launches and atmospheric reentry (including ozone-depleting pollutants)

**Additional risks**:
- Potential interference with asteroid detection during twilight observations
- Contribution to space debris accumulation

## Disputed Claims

The Starcloud 10× carbon reduction claim is directly contradicted by the Saarland University finding. Status: `contested` — requires a dedicated dispute section when this page is updated.
