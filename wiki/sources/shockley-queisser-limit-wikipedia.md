# Shockley–Queisser Limit (Wikipedia)

**Source**: Wikipedia
**Source type**: other
**Origin URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shockley%E2%80%93Queisser_limit
**Processing status**: processed
**Domain relevance**: supporting (theoretical ceiling for space solar power generation)

## Summary

The Shockley–Queisser (S-Q) limit defines the maximum thermodynamic efficiency
achievable by a single p-n junction solar cell. Multi-junction cells can exceed
this limit by capturing different spectral bands with multiple sub-cells, each
approaching its own S-Q limit.

## Key Claims

- **Single-junction S-Q limit (AM1.5)**: 33.16% for optimal bandgap (1.34 eV); ~32% for silicon (1.1 eV). — *(other)*
- **Single-junction original calculation**: ~30% at 1.1 eV (Shockley & Queisser, 1961). — *(other)*
- **Multi-junction limits (1-sun, unconcentrated)**:
  - 2-junction: ~42%
  - 3-junction: ~49%
  - Infinite junctions: ~68.7%
  — *(other)*
- **Multi-junction limit (concentrated sunlight)**: ~86.8% (infinite junctions). — *(other)*
- **Si commercial cells**: ~24% (well below theoretical ~32%). — *(other)*
- **Record (2023)**: LONGi Si/perovskite tandem achieved 33.9%, the first Si-based cell to exceed the single-junction S-Q limit. — *(other)*

## Significance for Space Data Centers

The infinite-junction limit of 68.7% at AM0 (1-sun in space) represents the
thermodynamic ceiling for space solar collection. Commercial triple-junction cells
(~28-30%) are operating at roughly 40-45% of that theoretical ceiling, indicating
substantial headroom in principle. Concentration (using mirrors/lenses) could push
practical systems toward 86.8%, but mass and complexity penalties in orbit are severe.
