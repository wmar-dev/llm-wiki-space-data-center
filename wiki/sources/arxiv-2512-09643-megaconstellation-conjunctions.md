# An Orbital House of Cards: Frequent Megaconstellation Close Conjunctions

**Authors**: Sarah Thiele, Skye R. Heiland, Aaron C. Boley, Samantha M. Lawler
**arXiv**: 2512.09643 (submitted Dec 10, 2025; revised Jan 8, 2026)
**Category**: Earth and Planetary Astrophysics
**Source type**: peer_reviewed (arXiv preprint; academic astrophysics)

## Summary

Introduces the **CRASH Clock** — a metric quantifying the expected time until a
catastrophic collision occurs if active satellite maneuvering and full situational
awareness are suspended.

## Key Findings

| Metric | 2018 | 2025 | Change |
|--------|------|------|--------|
| CRASH Clock | 164 days | **5.5 days** | **~30× compression** |

The ~30-fold reduction in the CRASH Clock over seven years reflects the rapid growth
of megaconstellations. In 2018, losing maneuvering capability would give 164 days
before a catastrophic collision was statistically expected. In 2025, the same failure
gives only **5.5 days**.

## Significance

The CRASH Clock makes the dependency on active avoidance *quantifiable*. The current
Starlink belt at 550 km operates safely only because:
1. SpaceX executes ~300,000 avoidance maneuvers/year (ESA 2026 data)
2. Full situational awareness is maintained (US SSN tracking + SpaceX autonomous avoidance)

If either condition fails — solar storm disabling avionics, software bug, company
bankruptcy, or adversarial interference — the expected time to a catastrophic collision
is ~5.5 days.

## Relevance for Orbital Data Centers

Any large non-maneuverable structure (a multi-ton data center with reduced agility)
would degrade the CRASH Clock further. The paper quantifies how megaconstellations
have consumed the "passive safety buffer" that once existed at LEO altitudes.
