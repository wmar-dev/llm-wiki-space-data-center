# The Economics of Outer Space (Bongers et al., Space Policy 2026)

**Authors**: Anelí Bongers, Benedetto Molinari, Sébastien Rouillon, José L. Torres
**Journal**: Space Policy (2026), article 101765
**DOI**: 10.1016/j.spacepol.2026.101765
**Received**: February 2025; accepted April 2026
**Access**: Open access (CC BY 4.0)
**Source type**: peer_reviewed

## Summary

Survey paper reviewing the economics of outer space — regulation, pollution, natural
resources, defense. Section 5 covers orbital debris economics. The paper synthesizes
the quantitative literature on debris thresholds and Kessler syndrome economics.

## Kessler Syndrome Economics (Section 5)

The paper synthesizes findings from the field:

- **Bongers & Torres (2023)** calibrate the threshold at **~72,000 satellites for all LEO**.
  In steady state, collision risk reduces satellite launches by 3.7% and the fleet by 4.5%
  vs. a no-debris world.
- **Adilov et al. (2018)** confirm: the economic Kessler threshold (space becomes
  unprofitable) occurs *before* the physical threshold (space becomes unusable).
- **Rao and Rondina**: estimate Earth's orbit could become unusable anywhere between
  **2040 and 2184** depending on model calibration. The range reflects deep uncertainty
  in debris decay rates and constellation growth projections.
- **Rao et al.**: under open access, the satellite industry's net present value ≈ **$600B**;
  under socially optimal policy it would be ≈ **$3,000B** — a **5× welfare gain** from
  proper debris regulation.
- **Adilov et al. (2022 simulation)**: debris stock increases at accelerating rate throughout
  the century, faster than NASA's reference LEGEND model — discrepancy attributed to
  endogenizing commercial launch incentives.

## Policy Conclusions

The paper argues timely regulatory intervention is essential. Current "first come, first
served" framework systematically overproduces satellites and debris relative to the social
optimum. Proposed instruments in the literature: launch taxes, cap-and-trade on debris
"slots", active debris removal (ADR) markets, combined with design standards.

## Relevance for Space Data Centers

- The 2040–2184 orbit-unusable range is the key risk window for any long-lived space
  infrastructure investment. A space data center constellation planned for 2030+
  would be deploying into an environment that models project could cascade by mid-century.
- The $600B vs $3,000B comparison shows the scale of the externality: open-access
  dynamics systematically degrade the shared orbital resource.
