# International Space Station Lithium-Ion Battery

**Authors**: Penni J. Dalton (NASA Glenn), Sonia Balcer (Aerojet Rocketdyne)
**Published**: April 18, 2016
**Source type**: peer_reviewed (NASA technical report)
**Origin URL**: https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20160012048

## Summary

Documents the ISS transition from Nickel-Hydrogen to Lithium-Ion batteries, including operational constraints of the Ni-H2 system and the rationale for the upgrade. Provides the authoritative DoD constraint figure for LEO battery operation.

## Key Claims

- ISS Ni-H2 batteries are designed to operate at a **maximum 35% depth of discharge (DoD)** during normal LEO operation — 65% of capacity held in reserve to extend cycle life
- 48 total Ni-H2 Orbital Replacement Units in service; oldest cycling since September 2006
- Batteries charge during sunlit periods and discharge during eclipse — the fundamental LEO power cycle constraint
- Li-ion development initiated in 2010 to replace aging Ni-H2 units

## Significance

The 35% DoD limit is the real-world constraint imposed by the need for multi-year LEO battery operation. Any orbital data center design must account for similar operational DoD limits, significantly reducing usable energy storage relative to rated capacity.
