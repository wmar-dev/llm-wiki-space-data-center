# Thermoelectric and Seebeck Effect Applications for Space

**Source**: Aggregated from MDPI (2023, 2016), ECSS handbook, ScienceDirect (2025)
**Source type**: other
**Processing status**: processed
**Ingest count**: 1

## Summary

Analysis of whether thermoelectric devices (Seebeck and Peltier effects) can be used for heat dissipation in space. Two distinct applications exist: thermoelectric cooling (Peltier) for active heat pumping, and thermoelectric generation (Seebeck) for waste heat recovery.

## Key Claims

- Thermoelectric coolers are solid-state heat pumps already flown on Mars Curiosity Rover (CCD cooling), ISS, and larger satellites. — *(other)*
- TECs can sometimes *increase* electronics temperature because I²R heat from the device itself adds to the thermal load. — *(other)*
- TEC cooling is beneficial only when paired with a very good (large) radiator; for CubeSats the required radiator area (~1.4 m²) is unrealistically large. — *(other)*
- TECs have much lower coefficient of performance than competing active cooling technologies. — *(other)*
- Temperature gradients inside small satellites available for Seebeck generation are <3 K — too small for meaningful power recovery with current technology. — *(other)*
- BiTe is the best thermoelectric material for satellite temperatures (<100°C). — *(other)*
- TEGs are maintenance-free, have no moving parts, and survive thermal cycling and vibration at medium loads. — *(other)*
- Diffusion barrier between copper contacts and functional BiTe material is a known weakness under thermal cycling. — *(other)*
- Integrated photothermal-thermoelectric-radiative cooling (PT-TE-RC) can achieve 18.96% conversion efficiency in deep space with temperature differences up to 3090 K. — *(other)*

## Domain Relevance

Directly relevant to space data center thermal management. Establishes that thermoelectrics are not a substitute for radiative cooling, and may be counterproductive for large heat loads.
